# Chapter 1 — What is Linear Algebra?

## The One-Sentence Answer

Linear algebra is the math of **vectors** (arrows) and **linear transformations** (ways of moving arrows around in predictable ways).

## Why Should You Care?

Linear algebra is the language of:

- **Neural networks** — every layer is a matrix multiplication
- **3D graphics** — rotating, scaling, projecting objects
- **Data analysis** — datasets are matrices, principal components are eigenvectors
- **Physics** — forces, velocities, quantum states are all vectors
- **Search engines** — Google's original algorithm (PageRank) is an eigenvector calculation

If you understand linear algebra, you hold one of the most powerful tools in all of applied math.

## The Two Perspectives

Every idea in linear algebra has **two ways** of looking at it:

1. **Numerical** — rows and columns of numbers, arithmetic rules
2. **Geometric** — arrows, grids, and spatial transformations

Most tutorials lean hard on numbers. This one leans hard on **geometry first, then numbers** — because the geometry is where the intuition lives.

## The Journey Ahead

We'll start with a single arrow (a vector). We'll learn to add arrows, scale them, and combine them. Then we'll introduce matrices — which you'll learn to see not as grids of numbers but as **instructions for transforming space**. Finally we'll reach the crown jewels: eigenvectors and their applications.

Ready? Let's go.

# Chapter 2 — Vectors — The Basic Object

## What is a Vector?

A vector is an **arrow in space**. It has two properties:

- **Direction** (which way it points)
- **Magnitude** (how long it is)

We draw vectors as arrows **starting at the origin** (the point (0, 0) in 2D). The arrow points to some other location — its "tip."

![A vector as an arrow](http://localhost:8000/books/7a55a92e-1f9b-4df7-b700-b4e640f1fbc4/images/01_vector_basics.png)

The vector v = (3, 2) is an arrow:

- Starting at the origin (0, 0)
- Ending at the point (3, 2)
- Which means it goes **3 units right and 2 units up**

## The Two Notations

Vectors can be written horizontally or vertically:

> **Row form:** v = (3, 2)

> **Column form:** $v = \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix}$

Both mean the same thing. Column form is more common in math textbooks; row form is easier to type. In this tutorial we'll mostly use row form, but switch to column form when working with matrices.

## Vector Components

The numbers inside a vector are called **components**. For v = (3, 2):

- The **first component** (also called the x-component) is 3
- The **second component** (also called the y-component) is 2

Each component tells you how far the vector goes in one direction.

## 3D and Higher-Dimensional Vectors

In 3D space, vectors have 3 components:

> v = (3, 2, 5)   means "go right 3, up 2, and forward 5"

In general, vectors can have any number of components. A vector with n components lives in **n-dimensional space** (often written ℝⁿ — "R to the n").

You can't visualize 100-dimensional space directly, but the algebra works the same way. When dealing with neural networks, we often work with vectors of thousands of components.

## The Zero Vector

There's one special vector: the **zero vector**, written **0** or 𝟎.

> 0 = (0, 0)   in 2D

It has no direction (it's just a point at the origin). It plays a role similar to the number 0 in regular arithmetic.

# Chapter 3 — Vector Operations

## Operation 1: Vector Addition

To add two vectors, add their components **one at a time**:

> If u = (3, 1) and v = (1, 3), then **u + v = (3+1, 1+3) = (4, 4)**

That's the arithmetic. But what does it MEAN geometrically?

### The Tip-to-Tail Rule

To add u + v geometrically: **place v's tail at u's tip, and the sum is the arrow from the origin to v's new tip.**

### The Parallelogram Rule

Same idea, different picture: **u + v is the diagonal of the parallelogram formed by u and v.**

![Vector addition](http://localhost:8000/books/7a55a92e-1f9b-4df7-b700-b4e640f1fbc4/images/02_vector_addition.png)

Both pictures give the same result. Use whichever makes sense to you.

### Why Add Component-by-Component?

Because "3 right then 1 right" is the same as "4 right total." The x-components add independently of the y-components. Each direction has its own independent sum.

## Operation 2: Scalar Multiplication

A **scalar** is just a regular number (not a vector). When you multiply a vector by a scalar, you multiply every component by that number:

> If v = (2, 1) and c = 3, then **3v = (6, 3)**

Geometrically, scalar multiplication does one of three things:

- **Positive scalar > 1**: stretches the vector longer
- **Positive scalar < 1**: shrinks the vector
- **Negative scalar**: flips the vector and stretches/shrinks it

![Scalar multiplication](http://localhost:8000/books/7a55a92e-1f9b-4df7-b700-b4e640f1fbc4/images/03_scalar_mult.png)

Notice: all the arrows **lie on the same line**. Scalar multiplication never changes a vector's direction — only its length (and possibly which way it points along that line).

## Operation 3: Vector Subtraction

Subtraction is just addition with a negative:

> u - v = u + (-1)v

**Example:** u = (5, 3), v = (2, 1). Then:

> u - v = (5-2, 3-1) = (3, 2)

Geometrically, u - v is the arrow that goes **from v's tip to u's tip**. Think of it as "the vector that, added to v, gives u."

## The Rules (Algebra of Vectors)

Vector operations follow the same rules as regular arithmetic:

- **Commutative:** u + v = v + u
- **Associative:** (u + v) + w = u + (v + w)
- **Distributive:** c(u + v) = cu + cv

# Chapter 4 — Linear Combinations and Span

## Linear Combinations

A **linear combination** of vectors u and v is any vector of the form:

> a·u + b·v

where a and b are any scalars (numbers).

**Example:** Let u = (1, 0) and v = (0, 1).

- 3u + 2v = (3, 0) + (0, 2) = **(3, 2)**
- -u + 5v = (-1, 0) + (0, 5) = **(-1, 5)**
- 0u + 0v = **(0, 0)** (the zero vector)
- 1.7u + (-3.1)v = **(1.7, -3.1)**

**A linear combination is just a scaled sum.** You scale each vector by some number, then add them.

## Span

The **span** of a set of vectors is the set of ALL possible linear combinations of them.

Think of it as: "Using these vectors as building blocks (with any scalar coefficients), what set of points can I reach?"

### Span of Two Vectors in 2D

**Case 1:** If u and v point in different directions, their span is the **whole 2D plane**. You can reach any point by combining them.

**Case 2:** If u and v point in the same (or opposite) direction, their span is just a **single line** through the origin.

![Span of vectors](http://localhost:8000/books/7a55a92e-1f9b-4df7-b700-b4e640f1fbc4/images/04_span.png)

On the left: any point in the plane can be reached.
On the right: only points on the line can be reached (because v is just a scaled u).

### Span in 3D

- **One vector** spans a line.
- **Two vectors** (pointing different directions) span a plane.
- **Three vectors** (all pointing different directions) span all of 3D space.

If two or more vectors happen to lie on the same line or plane, the span collapses to something smaller.

## Why Span Matters

Span answers a critical question: **"What can I build from these vectors?"**

In a neural network, for example, each neuron's output can be thought of as a linear combination of inputs. The span of these combinations determines what patterns the network can represent.

# Chapter 5 — Linear Independence

## The Question

Given a set of vectors, are any of them **redundant**?

A vector is redundant if it can be built from the others. If so, the set is **linearly dependent**. If not, it's **linearly independent**.

## Definition

A set of vectors is **linearly independent** if NO vector in the set can be written as a linear combination of the others.

Equivalently: the only way to combine them to get zero is to use all zero coefficients.

> a·u + b·v = 0   forces   a = b = 0

## Visual Intuition

![Linear dependence](http://localhost:8000/books/7a55a92e-1f9b-4df7-b700-b4e640f1fbc4/images/11_dependence.png)

- **Independent** (left): u and v point in different directions. Neither is a scaled version of the other. They span a full plane.
- **Dependent** (right): v = -1.5u, meaning v is just a scaled copy of u. They both lie on the same line, so they only span that line.

## Checking for Dependence — The Simple Test

For just two vectors: they're dependent if and only if **one is a scalar multiple of the other**.

**Examples:**

- u = (2, 4), v = (1, 2). Is v a scaled version of u? Yes: v = 0.5·u. **Dependent.**
- u = (1, 3), v = (2, 1). Is v a scaled version of u? If v = c·u, then 2 = c·1 and 1 = c·3, giving c = 2 and c = 1/3 — contradiction. **Independent.**

## Why Independence Matters

Independent vectors carry **new information**. Dependent vectors are redundant.

- 3 independent vectors in 3D → span all of 3D space
- 3 dependent vectors in 3D → might only span a plane (or even just a line)

This idea leads directly to the next chapter: basis.

# Chapter 6 — Basis and Dimension

## What is a Basis?

A **basis** for a space is a set of vectors that is:

1. **Linearly independent** (no redundancy)
2. **Spans the whole space** (enough to reach everywhere)

A basis is the smallest possible "toolkit" for building every vector in the space.

## The Standard Basis in 2D

The most famous basis in 2D is the **standard basis**:

> **î = (1, 0)**   (the "unit x-vector")
> **ĵ = (0, 1)**   (the "unit y-vector")

The little hats (î, ĵ) indicate they have length 1 — they're called **unit vectors**.

Any 2D vector can be written as a combination of î and ĵ:

> v = (3, 2) = 3î + 2ĵ

![Standard basis](http://localhost:8000/books/7a55a92e-1f9b-4df7-b700-b4e640f1fbc4/images/05_basis.png)

Every point in the plane is "3 steps right, 2 steps up" or similar — the components of a vector literally tell you how many copies of î and ĵ to add.

## The Standard Basis in 3D

> **î = (1, 0, 0)**, **ĵ = (0, 1, 0)**, **k̂ = (0, 0, 1)**

A 3D vector (a, b, c) = a·î + b·ĵ + c·k̂.

## Other Bases Exist

The standard basis isn't the ONLY basis. Any set of independent, spanning vectors works.

**Example:** In 2D, b₁ = (1, 1) and b₂ = (1, -1) form a different basis. Any 2D vector can be written as a combination of these two.

Different bases are like different coordinate systems for the same space. One of the deep insights of linear algebra is that **matrices let us translate between different bases.**

## Dimension

The **dimension** of a space is the number of vectors in any basis for it.

- 2D plane → dimension 2 (any basis has 2 vectors)
- 3D space → dimension 3
- n-dimensional space → dimension n

Dimension is an intrinsic property of the space — it doesn't depend on which basis you pick.

## Key Facts

- A basis for n-dimensional space always has exactly **n vectors**.
- If you have fewer than n vectors, you can't span the space.
- If you have more than n vectors, some must be dependent (redundant).

# Chapter 7 — Matrices — Numbers in a Grid

## Definition

A **matrix** is a rectangular grid of numbers. It has rows and columns.

$$A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 4 & 5 & 6 \end{pmatrix}$$

This matrix has **2 rows** and **3 columns**, so we call it a "2×3 matrix" (read "2 by 3").

## Individual Entries

Each number in a matrix is called an **entry** or **element**. We label entries by their (row, column) position:

- Entry at row 1, column 1: **1**
- Entry at row 1, column 2: **2**
- Entry at row 2, column 3: **6**

In formulas, we write the entry in row i and column j as **A_{ij}** (or A[i][j] in programming).

## Important Special Cases

**Row vector:** A 1×n matrix (single row)

$$\begin{pmatrix} 3 & 1 & 4 \end{pmatrix}$$

**Column vector:** An n×1 matrix (single column)

$$\begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ 1 \\ 4 \end{pmatrix}$$

Vectors are just matrices with one row or one column.

**Square matrix:** Same number of rows and columns (n×n).

**Identity matrix (I):** A square matrix with 1s on the diagonal and 0s elsewhere.

$$I = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$$

It plays the role of "the number 1" in matrix algebra (we'll see why in Chapter 10).

## Basic Operations on Matrices

**Matrix addition:** Add entries in matching positions. Both matrices must be the same size.

$$\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4 \end{pmatrix} + \begin{pmatrix} 5 & 6 \\ 7 & 8 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 6 & 8 \\ 10 & 12 \end{pmatrix}$$

**Scalar multiplication:** Multiply every entry by the scalar.

$$3 \cdot \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & 6 \\ 9 & 12 \end{pmatrix}$$

## The Transpose

The **transpose** of a matrix swaps its rows and columns. Written **Aᵀ**.

$$A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 4 & 5 & 6 \end{pmatrix} \quad \Rightarrow \quad A^T = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 4 \\ 2 & 5 \\ 3 & 6 \end{pmatrix}$$

Row 1 of A becomes column 1 of Aᵀ. Row 2 becomes column 2. And so on.

## But What Do Matrices MEAN?

Matrix addition and scalar multiplication are straightforward. The really deep idea — and the whole reason matrices exist — is that **a matrix represents a linear transformation**. That's the subject of the next chapter.

# Chapter 8 — Linear Transformations

## The Core Idea

A **linear transformation** is a way of moving every point in space to a new location, following two rules:

1. **Straight lines stay straight** (they don't curve)
2. **The origin stays put** (it doesn't move)

More precisely, a transformation T is linear if:

> T(u + v) = T(u) + T(v)   (additivity)
> T(c·v) = c·T(v)   (scaling)

In plain language: transforming first and then adding is the same as adding first and then transforming.

## Seeing a Transformation

The best way to understand a linear transformation is to watch what it does to the grid of space.

![Matrix transformation](http://localhost:8000/books/7a55a92e-1f9b-4df7-b700-b4e640f1fbc4/images/06_matrix_transform.png)

On the left: the original grid with î = (1, 0) and ĵ = (0, 1).

On the right: after the transformation, the grid has been rotated and stretched. î has landed at (2, 1) and ĵ has landed at (-1, 2). Everything else follows along.

## The Beautiful Trick: Track Only î and ĵ

Here's the big insight: **to describe a linear transformation completely, you only need to know where î and ĵ end up.**

Why? Because every vector is a combination of î and ĵ:

> v = (3, 2) = 3î + 2ĵ

After the transformation:

> T(v) = 3·T(î) + 2·T(ĵ)

(by the additivity and scaling rules). So if you know where î and ĵ go, you can compute where ANY vector goes.

**Example:** If T sends î → (2, 1) and ĵ → (-1, 2), then:

> T(3, 2) = 3·(2, 1) + 2·(-1, 2) = (6, 3) + (-2, 4) = (4, 7)

## From Transformation to Matrix

We package this info into a **matrix** by putting the new î as the first column and the new ĵ as the second column:

$$T = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & -1 \\ 1 & 2 \end{pmatrix}$$

Read the columns:

- First column (2, 1) = where î lands
- Second column (-1, 2) = where ĵ lands

**This is what a 2×2 matrix really is: a pair of instructions for where to send î and ĵ.** The rest of space follows.

## The Magic Summary

Matrices = linear transformations. They are two names for the same thing:

- **As numbers:** a matrix is a grid of entries
- **As geometry:** a matrix is an instruction for warping space linearly

# Chapter 9 — Matrix-Vector Multiplication

## The Formula

When you multiply a matrix by a vector, you apply the transformation to the vector.

$$\begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} ax + by \\ cx + dy \end{pmatrix}$$

## Why This Formula? (The Geometric Reason)

Recall: the columns of the matrix tell you where î and ĵ go.

- Column 1 = (a, c) = new î
- Column 2 = (b, d) = new ĵ

The vector (x, y) means "x copies of î plus y copies of ĵ." So after transformation:

> **New vector = x · (new î) + y · (new ĵ) = x·(a, c) + y·(b, d) = (ax + by, cx + dy)**

That's the formula. It's not arbitrary — it's literally "scale the columns by x and y, then add."

## Worked Example

Compute $\begin{pmatrix} 2 & -1 \\ 1 & 2 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix}$:

- First row: 2·3 + (-1)·2 = 6 - 2 = **4**
- Second row: 1·3 + 2·2 = 3 + 4 = **7**

Answer: $\begin{pmatrix} 4 \\ 7 \end{pmatrix}$

**Geometric check:** The matrix sends î → (2, 1) and ĵ → (-1, 2). So (3, 2) = 3î + 2ĵ goes to 3(2,1) + 2(-1,2) = (6,3) + (-2,4) = (4, 7). ✓

## Two Ways to Compute (Same Result)

**Way 1: Row-by-row (dot products)**

Take each row of the matrix and "dot" it with the vector. (We'll cover dot products formally in Chapter 15.)

**Way 2: Column-by-column (linear combination)**

Scale each column of the matrix by the corresponding vector entry, then add.

Way 1 is faster for manual calculation. Way 2 is better for geometric understanding. Learn both — they give identical answers.

## Dimensions Must Match

For Av to make sense, the number of **columns in A** must match the number of **entries in v**.

- A is m×n, v is n×1 (or n-dimensional) → Av is m×1
- 2×3 matrix × 3-vector = 2-vector ✓
- 2×3 matrix × 2-vector = **error** ✗

# Chapter 10 — Matrix-Matrix Multiplication

## The Meaning: Composition of Transformations

What happens if you apply one transformation after another? You get a **new transformation** — the combination.

If A and B are both transformations, then "apply B first, then apply A" is itself a linear transformation. And it's represented by the matrix product **AB**.

> (AB)v = A(Bv)   —   apply B to v, then apply A to the result

**This is the whole point of matrix multiplication: it represents composition.**

## How to Compute AB

The product AB has the property that **column j of AB = A times (column j of B)**.

So: to find AB, apply A to each column of B and stack the results.

### Worked Example

Let $A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4 \end{pmatrix}$ and $B = \begin{pmatrix} 5 & 6 \\ 7 & 8 \end{pmatrix}$.

**Find column 1 of AB:** A times column 1 of B = A times (5, 7).

$$A \begin{pmatrix} 5 \\ 7 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1·5 + 2·7 \\ 3·5 + 4·7 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 19 \\ 43 \end{pmatrix}$$

**Find column 2 of AB:** A times column 2 of B = A times (6, 8).

$$A \begin{pmatrix} 6 \\ 8 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1·6 + 2·8 \\ 3·6 + 4·8 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 22 \\ 50 \end{pmatrix}$$

**Assembled:**

$$AB = \begin{pmatrix} 19 & 22 \\ 43 & 50 \end{pmatrix}$$

## The Row-Times-Column Rule

A shortcut: the entry in row i, column j of AB equals the **dot product** of row i of A with column j of B.

Dot product = multiply corresponding entries, add them up.

(AB)₁₁ = (row 1 of A) · (column 1 of B) = 1·5 + 2·7 = 19 ✓

## Dimensions

For AB to make sense:

- A must be m×n
- B must be n×p (same middle dimension)
- The result AB is m×p

**The inner dimensions must match; the outer dimensions give the result's size.**

## Matrix Multiplication is NOT Commutative

Usually **AB ≠ BA**. Order matters! This makes sense geometrically: rotating and then scaling gives different results than scaling and then rotating.

## The Identity Matrix

The identity matrix I behaves like the number 1: **AI = IA = A** for any matrix A.

The identity transformation is "do nothing" — it sends î → î and ĵ → ĵ. Composing with "do nothing" leaves anything unchanged.

# Chapter 11 — Special Transformations

## The Four Classical Transformations

![Three classical transformations](http://localhost:8000/books/7a55a92e-1f9b-4df7-b700-b4e640f1fbc4/images/07_transformations.png)

### 1. Rotation

Rotates space by angle θ around the origin.

$$R_θ = \begin{pmatrix} \cos θ & -\sin θ \\ \sin θ & \cos θ \end{pmatrix}$$

**Example:** Rotate by 90°: $\begin{pmatrix} 0 & -1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$ (sends î → (0, 1) and ĵ → (-1, 0))

### 2. Scaling

Stretches or shrinks along each axis.

$$S = \begin{pmatrix} s_x & 0 \\ 0 & s_y \end{pmatrix}$$

- s_x = s_y → uniform scaling (all directions stretched equally)
- s_x ≠ s_y → non-uniform (different stretching per axis)

### 3. Shear

Tilts space along one direction.

$$Sh = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & k \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$$

This horizontal shear keeps î in place but pushes ĵ sideways by k.

### 4. Reflection

Flips space across a line.

$$F_x = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{pmatrix}$$

This reflects across the x-axis (flips ĵ upside down).

## Combining Transformations

Because matrix multiplication represents composition, you can **build complex transformations by multiplying simple ones**.

**Example:** Rotate by 45° AND THEN scale by 2. That's the matrix product (scale matrix) × (rotate matrix).

In 3D graphics and neural networks, entire sequences of transformations are collapsed into single matrices this way.

# Chapter 12 — The Determinant

## The Idea

The determinant is a single number that tells you how much a transformation **scales areas** (in 2D) or **volumes** (in 3D).

![Determinant as area scaling](http://localhost:8000/books/7a55a92e-1f9b-4df7-b700-b4e640f1fbc4/images/08_determinant.png)

The unit square (area 1) gets transformed into a parallelogram. If the new area is 3, then **det(A) = 3**.

## What the Number Means

- **det = 1**: transformation preserves area (e.g., rotations)
- **det = 2**: transformation doubles area
- **det = 0.5**: transformation halves area
- **det = 0**: transformation squashes space into a line (or point) — area becomes zero!
- **det < 0**: transformation flips orientation (like a reflection), in addition to scaling by the absolute value

## Computing the Determinant of a 2×2 Matrix

For a 2×2 matrix:

$$A = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix} \quad \Rightarrow \quad \det(A) = ad - bc$$

**Examples:**

- $\begin{pmatrix} 3 & 0 \\ 0 & 2 \end{pmatrix}$ → det = 3·2 - 0·0 = **6** (scales area by 6)

- $\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4 \end{pmatrix}$ → det = 1·4 - 2·3 = 4 - 6 = **-2** (flips + doubles)

- $\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 2 & 4 \end{pmatrix}$ → det = 1·4 - 2·2 = 0 (squashes to a line — note that columns are dependent!)

## The Critical Case: det = 0

When det = 0:

- The transformation squashes space into something lower-dimensional
- The columns of the matrix are linearly dependent
- The transformation is "non-invertible" — you can't undo it (information is lost)
- The matrix has no inverse

This will matter a lot when we solve equations in Chapter 13.

## 3D and Higher

In 3D, the determinant of a 3×3 matrix measures volume scaling. Computing it is more involved (it can be done via an expansion formula), but the meaning is the same.

## Properties to Know

- **det(AB) = det(A) · det(B)** — determinants multiply when you compose transformations.
- **det(I) = 1** — the identity doesn't change areas.
- **det(Aᵀ) = det(A)** — transpose doesn't change the determinant.

# Chapter 13 — Systems of Equations and Inverses

## Systems as Matrix Equations

A system of linear equations can be rewritten as a matrix equation.

**Example:**

> 2x + 3y = 7
> x + 4y = 6

Becomes:

$$\begin{pmatrix} 2 & 3 \\ 1 & 4 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 7 \\ 6 \end{pmatrix}$$

Or in compact form:

> **Ax = b**

where A is the coefficient matrix, x is the unknown vector, and b is the right-hand side.

## The Geometric Meaning

**Ax = b asks: "What vector x gets transformed into b by A?"**

We know where A sends the standard basis. The question is: which starting vector ends up at b?

## The Inverse Matrix

If A is "invertible" (det ≠ 0), there's a matrix **A⁻¹** that undoes A. Multiplying by A⁻¹ reverses the transformation.

Given Ax = b, if we multiply both sides by A⁻¹:

> A⁻¹Ax = A⁻¹b
> Ix = A⁻¹b
> **x = A⁻¹b**

That's the solution! Multiply both sides by the inverse.

## Computing the 2×2 Inverse

For a 2×2 matrix $A = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix}$, the inverse is:

$$A^{-1} = \frac{1}{\det(A)} \begin{pmatrix} d & -b \\ -c & a \end{pmatrix}$$

Steps: swap the diagonal entries, negate the off-diagonal entries, divide everything by the determinant.

**Example:** $A = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 3 \\ 1 & 4 \end{pmatrix}$

det(A) = 2·4 - 3·1 = 5

$$A^{-1} = \frac{1}{5} \begin{pmatrix} 4 & -3 \\ -1 & 2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 4/5 & -3/5 \\ -1/5 & 2/5 \end{pmatrix}$$

**Verify:** $A \cdot A^{-1}$ should equal I. Try it — it works.

## When There's No Inverse

If **det(A) = 0**, the matrix is **singular** (non-invertible). Geometrically, A squashes space to a lower dimension — there's no way to "unsquash" back.

In this case, Ax = b has:

- **No solution** (if b isn't in the squashed space), OR
- **Infinitely many solutions** (if b happens to lie in the squashed space)

## Gaussian Elimination (The Practical Method)

For large systems, computing inverses is slow. The practical method is **Gaussian elimination**:

1. Write the system as an augmented matrix [A | b].
2. Use row operations to transform it into [I | x], reading off the solution.

Row operations allowed:

- Swap two rows
- Multiply a row by a nonzero constant
- Add a multiple of one row to another

This method works regardless of whether the matrix is invertible, and handles any system.

# Chapter 14 — Rank and Null Space

## Rank — The "Output Dimension"

The **rank** of a matrix is the dimension of its output space — i.e., the dimension of the span of its columns.

- If the columns are all independent → **full rank**
- If some columns are redundant → **less than full rank**

**Examples:**

- $\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$ has rank 2 (columns are independent, span all of 2D)
- $\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 2 & 4 \end{pmatrix}$ has rank 1 (columns are multiples of each other, span a line)
- $\begin{pmatrix} 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$ has rank 0 (everything squashes to origin)

Rank tells you **how much "room" the transformation actually fills**. A rank-2 matrix in 2D fills all of 2D. A rank-1 matrix in 2D only fills a line.

## Null Space — What Gets Squashed to Zero

The **null space** (or kernel) of A is the set of all vectors v such that **Av = 0**.

- For an invertible matrix, the only vector that maps to 0 is 0 itself. Null space = {0}.
- For a non-invertible matrix, a whole line (or plane) of vectors maps to 0. The null space has dimension ≥ 1.

## The Rank-Nullity Theorem

For an n-dimensional input space:

> **rank + nullity = n**

(where nullity = dimension of null space).

This is a conservation law: dimensions lost from the null space equal dimensions kept in the output.

## Why This Matters

Rank and null space let you fully characterize what a transformation does:

- **Full rank (rank = n)**: nothing is squashed, transformation is invertible
- **Rank < n**: some dimensions are squashed, information is lost

In neural networks, the rank of weight matrices relates to how expressive a layer is.

# Chapter 15 — The Dot Product

## Definition

The **dot product** of two vectors is a single number:

> u · v = u₁v₁ + u₂v₂ + ... + uₙvₙ

Multiply matching components, add them up.

**Example:** u = (3, 1, 2) and v = (4, 0, 5)

> u · v = 3·4 + 1·0 + 2·5 = 12 + 0 + 10 = **22**

## Two Meanings

### 1. Geometric Meaning

> **u · v = |u| |v| cos θ**

where |u| and |v| are the lengths, and θ is the angle between them.

![Dot product and projections](http://localhost:8000/books/7a55a92e-1f9b-4df7-b700-b4e640f1fbc4/images/10_dotproduct.png)

- **u · v > 0**: angle is less than 90° (vectors point similar directions)
- **u · v = 0**: angle is exactly 90° (vectors are **perpendicular/orthogonal**)
- **u · v < 0**: angle is more than 90° (vectors point opposite-ish directions)

### 2. Projection Meaning

u · v is also related to the **projection** of v onto u. The dot product measures "how much of v lies along the direction of u."

## Length from Dot Product

The length (magnitude) of a vector can be computed from its dot product with itself:

> **|v|² = v · v**

So |v| = √(v · v).

**Example:** v = (3, 4) → v · v = 9 + 16 = 25 → |v| = 5.

## Unit Vectors

A **unit vector** has length 1. To make any vector into a unit vector (same direction, length 1), divide by its length:

> **û = v / |v|**

This is called **normalization**. Unit vectors are used everywhere — they let you separate "direction" from "magnitude."

# Chapter 16 — Projections and Orthogonality

## Projection — The "Shadow"

The **projection of v onto u** is the "shadow" of v cast onto the line through u.

Formula:

> **proj_u(v) = (u · v / u · u) · u**

In words: scale u by the number (u·v)/(u·u).

**Example:** u = (4, 0), v = (3, 2)

> u · v = 12 + 0 = 12
> u · u = 16 + 0 = 16
> proj_u(v) = (12/16) · (4, 0) = 0.75 · (4, 0) = **(3, 0)**

That makes sense: v's shadow on the x-axis (direction of u) has x-component 3 (same as v) and y-component 0.

## Orthogonality

Two vectors are **orthogonal** (perpendicular) if:

> **u · v = 0**

This is the single most useful fact in all of linear algebra. Orthogonality makes everything clean.

## Why Orthogonality is So Useful

An **orthogonal basis** (basis vectors that are all mutually perpendicular, and all unit length — called **orthonormal**) makes everything easy:

- Coordinates are just dot products: v · î = x-component
- No "skew" to worry about
- Calculations become direct

The standard basis {î, ĵ, k̂} is orthonormal, which is why it's so convenient.

## The Gram-Schmidt Process (Named Drop)

If you have any basis, there's a procedure (Gram-Schmidt) that turns it into an orthonormal basis. It's a sequence of projections and subtractions. You don't need to memorize it, but know that it exists.

# Chapter 17 — Eigenvalues and Eigenvectors

## The Big Idea

When a matrix transforms space, most vectors get knocked off their original line — they rotate to point somewhere new.

But some special vectors **stay on their own line**. They might get stretched or shrunk, but they don't rotate. These are **eigenvectors**.

![Eigenvectors](http://localhost:8000/books/7a55a92e-1f9b-4df7-b700-b4e640f1fbc4/images/09_eigenvectors.png)

Look at the picture: most vectors (gray) get moved to new directions. But the two special lines (red and green dashes) contain vectors that, when transformed, just scale along their own lines.

## Formal Definition

A vector v is an **eigenvector** of matrix A if:

> **A v = λ v**

for some scalar λ. The number λ is called the **eigenvalue** associated with v.

Read this as: **"applying A to v is the same as just stretching v by λ."**

- λ = 2 → v stretches to double length
- λ = 0.5 → v shrinks to half length
- λ = 1 → v is unchanged
- λ = -1 → v flips to opposite direction
- λ = 0 → v collapses to the origin

## Why Are Eigenvectors Important?

Eigenvectors are the **"natural axes"** of a transformation. They reveal the matrix's fundamental behavior — stripped of all complication.

A matrix might look complicated in general coordinates. But viewed in eigenvector coordinates, it's just "stretch by λ₁ along one axis, and stretch by λ₂ along another." Simple!

This is used in:

- **Principal Component Analysis (PCA)** — find the eigenvectors of a data covariance matrix to identify the most important directions in data
- **Google's PageRank** — the ranking is the eigenvector of a certain matrix
- **Physics** — quantum states, vibration modes
- **Neural networks** — weight matrix eigenvalues affect training stability

## Finding Eigenvalues

The equation Av = λv can be rewritten:

> (A - λI) v = 0

For this to have a nonzero solution, (A - λI) must be singular:

> **det(A - λI) = 0**

This is the **characteristic equation**. Solve it for λ.

### Worked Example

Find eigenvalues of $A = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 \end{pmatrix}$.

$$A - \lambda I = \begin{pmatrix} 2-\lambda & 1 \\ 1 & 2-\lambda \end{pmatrix}$$

Determinant: (2-λ)(2-λ) - 1·1 = (2-λ)² - 1 = 0

Expanding: 4 - 4λ + λ² - 1 = 0 → λ² - 4λ + 3 = 0

Factoring: (λ - 1)(λ - 3) = 0

**Eigenvalues: λ = 1 and λ = 3.**

## Finding Eigenvectors

For each eigenvalue, solve (A - λI)v = 0.

**For λ = 3:** A - 3I = $\begin{pmatrix} -1 & 1 \\ 1 & -1 \end{pmatrix}$

The equation (A - 3I)v = 0 gives -x + y = 0, or y = x. So any vector (t, t) works. Take **v₁ = (1, 1)**.

**For λ = 1:** A - I = $\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$

The equation gives x + y = 0, or y = -x. Take **v₂ = (1, -1)**.

Summary: A has eigenvalues {1, 3} with eigenvectors {(1, -1), (1, 1)}.

## Diagonalization (Optional Preview)

If a matrix has a full set of independent eigenvectors, it can be **diagonalized**:

> A = PDP⁻¹

where D is a diagonal matrix of eigenvalues and P has the eigenvectors as columns. This is wildly useful because diagonal matrices are trivial to work with — you can compute Aⁿ just by raising D to the nth power.

# Chapter 18 — Linear Algebra in Neural Networks

You made it! Let's see how everything you learned shows up in neural networks.

## A Neural Network Layer

A single layer of a neural network computes:

> **output = activation( W · x + b )**

Where:

- **x** is the input vector (say, 784 pixel values for an MNIST digit)
- **W** is a weight matrix (say, 128 × 784)
- **b** is a bias vector (length 128)
- **W · x** is matrix-vector multiplication (Chapter 9)
- **activation** is a nonlinear function (like sigmoid)

The result is a vector of 128 numbers. **This is just linear algebra plus a nonlinearity.**

## What the Matrix W Does

Geometrically, W is a linear transformation (Chapter 8). It takes the 784-dimensional input space and transforms it into a 128-dimensional feature space. Each output neuron is a dot product (Chapter 15) of the input with a row of W.

## Why Nonlinearity?

If you just stacked matrix multiplications, you'd get another matrix multiplication (composition of linear transformations is linear — Chapter 10). The network would be useless.

By inserting a nonlinear activation function (like sigmoid) between matrix multiplications, you get something fundamentally new — a universal function approximator.

## Backpropagation and Gradients

Training a network means computing derivatives of the loss with respect to the weights. These derivatives are organized into **gradient vectors** and **Jacobian matrices**. The chain rule for matrix-valued functions is a central tool.

## Why Eigenvalues Matter for Training

The eigenvalues of weight matrices affect:

- **Vanishing/exploding gradients** — eigenvalues much less than 1 cause gradients to vanish; much greater than 1 cause explosion
- **Training stability** — well-conditioned matrices (eigenvalues in a good range) train smoothly
- **Initialization strategies** — schemes like Xavier and He initialization are designed to keep weight matrices well-conditioned

## Dimensions Are Everything

Most neural network bugs are dimension mismatches. If W is 128×784 and you try to multiply by a 100-dimensional input, the computation breaks (Chapter 9: inner dimensions must match).

Getting comfortable with "a 128×784 matrix times a 784-vector gives a 128-vector" is essential.

## The Payoff

Every neural network you've seen is, under the hood, a sequence of:

1. Linear transformations (matrix multiplications)
2. Nonlinear activations (element-wise functions)
3. Gradient-based optimization (derivatives of compositions)

You now have all the tools to understand how it works.
